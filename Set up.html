(i) Soapy detergents

Amina lives in a rural community where most households use hard water from a protected well. One day, while washing clothes, she accidentally dirtied her white blouse with mud. She decided to clean it using an ordinary soap. After washing and rinsing several times, brown stains remained on the blouse.
Task:

a) Identify the mistake Amina made when choosing the cleaning product.
b) Explain how the cleaning product works.
c) Advise Amina on the challenges associated with long-term use of the product.
(d) Evaluate the product used.

Response

a) Mistake made when choosing the cleaning product
Amina made the mistake of using soap (a soapy detergent) instead of a soapless detergent while washing with hard water from a well.


Point to note:

Hard water contains calcium and magnesium ions which react with soap to form an insoluble scum. This scum reduces the cleaning action of soap and causes brown stains to remain on clothes even after thorough rinsing.


(b) How the cleaning product works

Dirt on clothes is usually held by a thin layer of grease or oil. Cleaning agents such as soaps and detergents remove dirt through a process called emulsification.

A soap molecule consists of two distinct parts:
‚Ä¢ A hydrophilic (polar) head, which is soluble in water.
‚Ä¢ A hydrophobic (non-polar) tail, which is soluble in grease and oil.

During washing, soap reduces the surface tension between water and grease. The hydrophobic tail embeds itself into the oily dirt on the cloth, while the hydrophilic head remains in the water.

When the cloth is agitated, the grease is broken into tiny droplets surrounded by soap molecules.

These droplets are dispersed in water as an emulsion and are removed during rinsing, leaving the cloth clean.

(c) Challenges associated with long-term use of the product
I. Prolonged use of soap may lead to skin irritation, burns or blisters, due to the alkaline nature of soaps and detergents.
Mitigation measures
‚Ä¢ Wash hands and skin thoroughly with clean water after washing clothes.
‚Ä¢ Use protective gloves when washing frequently.
II. Prolonged use of soap may also lead to eye irritation, redness and pain if the detergent comes into contact with the eyes, which may result in temporary or permanent eye damage (loss of vision).
Mitigation measures
‚Ä¢ In case of eye contact, rinse immediately with plenty of clean water and seek medical attention if necessary.

(d) Evaluation of the products
(i) Similarities between soapy and soapless detergents
‚Ä¢ Both are salts of long-chain organic acids.
‚Ä¢ Both remove dirt by emulsification.
‚Ä¢ Both are effective cleansing agents in soft water.
(ii) Differences between soapy detergents and soapless detergents
‚Ä¢ Soapy detergents react with hard water to form scum while soapless do not form scum in hard or soft water.
‚Ä¢ Soapy detergents are gentle on the skin whereas soapless may be harsh to the skin on prolonged use.
‚Ä¢ Soapy detergents are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids while soapless are sodium salts of long-chain benzene sulphonic acids.
‚Ä¢ Soapy detergents cannot be used in strongly acidic solutions whereas soapless can be used in acidic solutions.
‚Ä¢ Soapy detergents are biodegradable and environmentally friendly while soapless are mostly non-biodegradable and may cause environmental pollution.
Conclusion / Advice
Therefore, Amina is advised to use soapless detergents when washing with hard water, as they clean effectively without forming scum and leaving stains on clothes.

Elements, compounds, and mixtures

(i) Elements

In a trading centre near Luwero, villagers are setting up a small workshop and a community health unit. They use iron to make tools and roofing sheets, oxygen at the health unit, and silicon in solar panels installed to provide electricity. Some villagers do not understand how these substances differ or why different materials are chosen for different purposes. They are also concerned about the effects of using these substances on the environment. They have therefore consulted you, as a student of chemistry, for help.


Task
As a student of chemistry, help the villagers to:
(a) Explain the category of each element used.
(b) Explain the properties of the elements that make them suitable for their uses.
(c) State the uses of each element in daily life.
(d) Explain one environmental impact of using each one of the elements and suggest a mitigation measure.

Response

(a) Category of the elements

Iron (Fe)

Iron is a metal.
Reason: It loses electrons from its outermost shell to form positive ions (cations).

Oxygen (O)


Oxygen is a non-metal.
Reason: It gains electrons during chemical reactions to form negative ions or shares electrons in covalent bonding.

Silicon (Si)


Silicon is a metalloid (semi-metal).
Reason: It shows properties intermediate between metals and non-metals and mainly forms covalent bonds.

(b) Properties of the elements


Iron


  • ‚Ä¢ Has a high melting point
  • ‚Ä¢ Conducts heat and electricity
  • ‚Ä¢ Is malleable and ductile
  • ‚Ä¢ Is hard and strong
  • ‚Ä¢ Has a shiny surface when polished

Oxygen


  • ‚Ä¢ Exists as a gas at room temperature
  • ‚Ä¢ Supports combustion
  • ‚Ä¢ Is slightly soluble in water
  • ‚Ä¢ Is chemically reactive with many elements

Silicon


  • ‚Ä¢ Has a high melting point
  • ‚Ä¢ Is a poor conductor of electricity at room temperature but conducts when heated or doped
  • ‚Ä¢ Is hard and brittle
  • ‚Ä¢ Has a shiny appearance

(c) Uses of the elements


Iron


  • ‚Ä¢ Used in making tools, roofing sheets and construction materials
  • ‚Ä¢ Used in the manufacture of machines and vehicles

Oxygen


  • ‚Ä¢ Used for breathing in hospitals
  • ‚Ä¢ Used in welding and metal cutting
  • ‚Ä¢ Used in combustion processes in industries

Silicon


  • ‚Ä¢ Used in the manufacture of solar panels
  • ‚Ä¢ Used in making electronic components such as transistors and computer chips
  • ‚Ä¢ Used in the manufacture of glass when combined with oxygen

(d) Environmental impact and mitigation (for all three elements)


Iron


Impact: Rusting of iron structures leads to environmental waste and weak structures.
Mitigation: Painting, oiling or galvanising iron to prevent rusting.

Oxygen


Impact: High oxygen concentration can increase the rate of fires and combustion.
Mitigation: Proper storage and controlled use of oxygen cylinders.

Silicon


Impact: Mining of silicon (sand) can cause land degradation and soil erosion.
Mitigation: Controlled mining and land reclamation after mining activities.

(ii) Compounds

In a small industrial village, three workplaces use different substances in their operations. A food-processing factory uses sodium chloride for preserving and seasoning foods, a chemical laboratory uses carbon dioxide gas for experiments and fire extinguishers, and a metal workshop uses copper for wiring and machinery parts. Some residents mistakenly think all these substances are elements and are concerned about safety and environmental impacts. They have approached you, a student of chemistry, to clarify the situation.


Task
As a chemistry learner, help the residents to;
(a) explain the category and nature of each compound.
(b) describe how each compound is formed.
(c) explain two properties of each compound based on its structure.
(d) state one use of each compound in daily life or industry.
(e) explain one environmental or health impact associated with each compound and mitigation.

Response

(a) Explain the category of each compound

  • ‚Ä¢ Sodium chloride (NaCl): Ionic compound, formed by complete transfer of electrons from a metal (sodium) to a non-metal (chlorine) creating oppositely charged ions.
  • ‚Ä¢ Carbon dioxide CO2: Covalent compound, formed by sharing electrons between two non-metal atoms (carbon and oxygen) to achieve stable electron configurations.
  • ‚Ä¢ Copper (Cu): Metallic compound (elemental metal), composed of metal atoms in a lattice with delocalized electrons forming metallic bonds.

(b) Explain the properties of the compounds based on their structures

Sodium chloride:

  • o High melting and boiling points due to strong electrostatic forces between ions.
  • o Soluble in water and conducts electricity when molten or in solution.
  • o Exists as a crystalline solid at room temperature.

Carbon dioxide:

  • o Low melting and boiling points due to weak van der Waals forces between molecules.
  • o Does not conduct electricity because there are no free ions.
  • o Exists as a gas at room temperature.

Copper:

  • o High melting and boiling points due to strong metallic bonding.
  • o Good conductor of heat and electricity because of delocalized electrons.
  • o Malleable and ductile, can be drawn into wires or sheets.

(c) State one use of each compound

  • ‚Ä¢ Sodium chloride: Used in food processing and preservation, seasoning foods.
  • ‚Ä¢ Carbon dioxide: Used in fire extinguishers and chemical laboratory experiments.
  • ‚Ä¢ Copper: Used in electrical wiring, machinery, and plumbing.

(d) Explain one environmental or health impact of each compound and suggest a mitigation measure

  • ‚Ä¢ Sodium chloride: Excess use can lead to soil salinity, affecting crop growth.
  • Mitigation:
    Regulate usage and dispose of excess salt safely.
  • ‚Ä¢ Carbon dioxide: Accumulation in the atmosphere contributes to greenhouse effect and global warming.
  • Mitigation: Capture and store CO2 emissions and promote afforestation.
  • ‚Ä¢ Copper: Improper disposal of copper waste can contaminate soil and water, harming aquatic life.
  • Mitigation: Recycle copper scraps and treat industrial effluents before release.
